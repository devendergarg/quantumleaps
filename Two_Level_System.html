About this Simulation

This application is an interactive tool for exploring the fundamental principles of Statistical Mechanics. It simulates a system of particles that can exist in one of two energy levels (a "two-level system") while in contact with a large heat reservoir at a constant temperature (a Canonical Ensemble).

The core of the simulation is the competition between two statistical ideas:

  • Energy Cost: Systems tend to prefer lower energy states. The probability of occupying a higher energy state is penalized by a term called the Boltzmann Factor, exp(-ΔE / kBT). This penalty is severe at low temperatures but becomes less important as temperature rises.
  • Entropy / Multiplicity: Systems tend to prefer states that can be arranged in the most number of ways. The "System Gain" (Ω) counts these arrangements (microstates). This factor favors disorder and distributions where particles are spread out.

Particle Types

The rules for counting microstates depend on whether the particles are distinguishable or not, leading to three different kinds of statistics:

  • Distinguishable: These are classical particles that can be individually labeled. The counting follows Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics.
  • Bosons: These are indistinguishable quantum particles (like photons) that are allowed to occupy the same quantum state. They follow Bose-Einstein statistics and have a tendency to "bunch up" in the same state.
  • Fermions: These are indistinguishable quantum particles (like electrons) that must obey the Pauli Exclusion Principle—no two fermions can occupy the same quantum state. They follow Fermi-Dirac statistics.

Controls Explained

  • Degeneracy (g₀, g₁): This is the number of distinct states available at the same energy level. For Fermions, this sets the maximum number of particles that can occupy that level.
  • Particle Visualization: Shows the average number of particles in each level, rounded to the nearest integer.
  • Probability Chart: Displays the full probability distribution for finding the system with N₁ particles in the excited state.
  • Table View: Allows you to inspect the raw "Factors" (System Gain vs. Reservoir Cost) or the final "Probability" for each possible state. The most probable state is highlighted.
